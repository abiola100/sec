OCCUPATION



occupation can be define as a person's regular job or profession. For example, a person's occupation might be a teacher, doctor, engineer, or farmer.




DIVISION OF OCCUPATIONS


  1. Primary Occupations: These involve the extraction and production of natural resources. Examples include agriculture, mining, fishing, and forestry.





  2. Secondary Occupations: These involve manufacturing and processing activities. Examples include construction, factories, and crafts.




  3. Tertiary Occupations: These involve providing services rather than goods. Examples include retail, healthcare, education, and transportation.


FACTOR WHICH AFFECT OCCUPATION


  • Education and Training : The level of education and specialized training a person receives greatly influences their occupation. Higher education often opens doors to more advanced and higher-paying jobs, while specific training or certifications can qualify someone for specialized roles within a field.

  • Economic Conditions: The Conditions of the economy can impact job availability and security. During economic booms, there may be more job opportunities and higher wages, while economic downturns can lead to job losses and increased competition for available positions.

  • Technological Advancements: technology can create new occupations and render some obsolete. For instance, the rise of digital marketing has created demand for roles in social media management and data analysis, while traditional roles in print media may decline.

  • Geographic Location: The availability of certain occupations can vary based on location. For example, a city with a strong technology sector might offer many tech jobs, while a rural area might have more opportunities in agriculture or manufacturing.

  • Personal Interests and Skills: An individual's interests and innate skills can guide their choice of occupation. People often pursue careers that align with their passions and strengths, which can lead to greater job satisfaction and success in their chosen field.

  • Societal Trends and Demands: Shifts in societal needs and preferences can affect job markets. For example, growing awareness of environmental issues has increased demand for careers in sustainability and green technologies.

  • Government Policies and Regulations: Policies and regulations can influence job opportunities by affecting certain industries. For example, subsidies for renewable energy can boost jobs in that sector, while restrictions on certain practices can lead to a decline in related occupations.


DIRECT AND INDIRECT SERVICE


DIRECT SERVICE


Direct services involve immediate, personal interaction between the service provider and the recipient. These services are provided firsthand and are directly received by the consumer.

Examples A doctor treating a patient, A teacher instructing students in a classroom, A salesperson assisting a customer in a store, A waiter serving food to customers in a restaurant.


INDIRECT SERVICE


Indirect services are those that help support or make direct services possible. They usually don't meet or talk to the people who use the service.

Example Someone who organizes schedules and manages supplies for a company , A person who fixes and maintains the computers that employees use , A person who manages how products are delivered to stores.
