FARM ANIMAL
Meaning and Definition of Farm Animals

Farm animals, also called livestock, are animals that are reared and managed by humans for different purposes such as food, income, work, protection, or companionship. They are different from wild animals because they rely on humans for shelter, feeding, and breeding.

Examples: Cattle, goats, sheep, pigs, rabbits, poultry birds, and fish.



Forms (Types) of Farm Animals

Farm animals can be grouped according to their main uses:

  1. Work animals: Used to perform tasks such as ploughing, carrying loads, or pulling carts.
    Examples: Donkeys, camels, oxen.

  2. Dairy animals: Animals kept mainly for milk production.
    Examples: Cows, goats, sometimes sheep.

  3. Meat (beef) animals: Animals kept primarily for meat production.
    Examples: Cattle, sheep, goats, pigs.

  4. Poultry birds: Domesticated birds kept for eggs or meat.
    Examples: Chickens, ducks, guinea fowls, turkeys.

  5. Guard animals: Animals used for protection of people or property.
    Examples: Dogs.

  6. Pet animals: Animals kept for companionship or pleasure.
    Examples: Certain dogs, cats, rabbits, fish.

  7. Aquatic animals: Animals kept in water for food or other purposes.
    Examples: Fish like catfish and tilapia.


Classification of Farm Animals

Farm animals can be classified in different ways based on characteristics, use, habitat, reproduction, and digestive system. Understanding classification helps farmers, students, and agricultural workers identify and manage animals properly.

1. Classification by Size

Farm animals can be grouped according to their body size:

  • Large animals: These are animals with big body size and usually require more feed and space.
    Examples: Cattle, horses, camels.

  • Small animals: These are animals with small body size and require less feed and space.
    Examples: Rabbits, poultry birds (chickens, ducks), fish.

2. Classification by Habitat

Animals can also be classified based on where they live or are adapted to live:

  • Terrestrial animals: Animals that live primarily on land.
    Examples: Cattle, goats, sheep, pigs.

  • Aquatic animals: Animals that live in water or require water for their survival.
    Examples: Fish like catfish and tilapia, frogs.

3. Classification by Reproduction

Farm animals can be divided based on how they reproduce:

  • Mammals: These animals give birth to live young and feed them with milk produced by the mother.
    Examples: Cattle, goats, sheep, pigs, rabbits.

  • Non-mammals: These animals lay eggs instead of giving birth to live young.
    Examples: Poultry birds (chickens, ducks, guinea fowls), fish.

4. Classification by Digestive System

Farm animals can also be classified based on the structure of their stomachs:

  • Ruminants: Animals with a complex stomach divided into four compartments (rumen, reticulum, omasum, abomasum). They chew cud to aid digestion.
    Examples: Cattle, goats, sheep, deer.

  • Non-ruminants (monogastric): Animals with a simple stomach; they do not chew cud.
    Examples: Pigs, poultry birds, rabbits, horses.

5. Classification by Purpose or Use

Farm animals can also be grouped according to why they are reared:

  1. Work animals: Used for carrying loads, ploughing, or other manual tasks.
    Examples: Donkeys, camels, oxen.

  2. Dairy animals: Kept mainly for milk production.
    Examples: Cows, goats, sheep.

  3. Meat animals: Raised primarily for meat.
    Examples: Cattle, pigs, goats, sheep.

  4. Poultry birds: Raised for eggs or meat.
    Examples: Chickens, ducks, turkeys, guinea fowls.

  5. Guard animals: Animals that protect homes, property, or livestock.
    Examples: Dogs.

  6. Pet animals: Animals kept for companionship.
    Examples: Dogs, cats, rabbits, ornamental birds.

  7. Aquatic animals: Animals kept in water for food or other purposes.
    Examples: Fish like catfish and tilapia.


Characteristics of Selected Farm Animals

1. Cattle

  1. Large, hoofed mammals. Some breeds have humps (e.g., Zebu), others are humpless.

  2. Both males (bulls) and females (cows) may have horns.

  3. Used for meat, milk, leather, work (ploughing or transport), and income.

  4. Ruminants (chew cud and have a four-compartment stomach).

  5. Social animals, usually kept in herds.

2. Goat

  1. Medium-sized mammals with horns; males often have beards and a strong smell.

  2. Hardy and adaptable to various environments.

  3. Kept for meat, milk, and skin.

  4. Ruminants with four stomach compartments.

  5. Can survive on low-quality forage better than many other farm animals.

3. Sheep

  1. Hoofed mammals, usually covered with wool.

  2. Females (ewes) are generally hornless, males (rams) may have horns.

  3. Raised for meat (mutton), wool, and skin.

  4. Ruminants with four-compartment stomachs.

  5. Social and graze in flocks.

4. Pig

  1. Medium-sized hoofed mammals with a snout used for rooting in the soil.

  2. Do not sweat; wallowing in mud helps to regulate body temperature.

  3. Raised mainly for meat (pork).

  4. Non-ruminants (monogastric) with a simple stomach.

  5. Intelligent animals and can adapt to intensive management systems.

5. Poultry Birds

  1. Domesticated birds like chickens, ducks, turkeys, and guinea fowls.

  2. They have feathers, wings, beaks, and lay eggs.

  3. Raised for eggs, meat, and sometimes ornamental purposes.

  4. Non-ruminants (monogastric) with a simple digestive system.

  5. They are social and can be kept in large flocks.

6. Rabbit

  1. Small mammals with long ears, fur-covered bodies, and strong hind legs.

  2. Raised for meat, fur, and sometimes as pets.

  3. Non-ruminants (monogastric) with a simple stomach.

  4. Highly reproductive and can breed multiple times a year.

  5. Require clean housing and soft bedding for comfort.

7. Donkey

  1. Small to medium-sized hoofed mammals with long ears.

  2. Used mainly as a work animal for carrying loads or pulling carts.

  3. Hardy and able to survive in harsh conditions with minimal feed.

  4. Non-ruminants (simple stomach).

  5. Calm, patient, and can carry heavy loads relative to body size.

8. Fish (Aquatic Animals)

  1. Live in water and have gills for breathing.

  2. Raised mainly for food (protein source).

  3. Adapted to aquatic life with fins for swimming and scales for protection.

  4. Non-ruminants (simple digestive system).

  5. Require well-maintained water environments for proper growth.


Uses of Farm Animals
  1. Food production: Meat, milk, eggs.

  2. Work: Some animals are used to plough land or transport goods.

  3. Income: Selling animals or animal products.

  4. By-products: Hides, skins, feathers, wool, manure.

  5. Protection: Guarding property or livestock.

  6. Companionship: Pets for pleasure and social purposes.

  7. Environmental benefits: Manure fertilizes soil, grazing controls weeds.

  8. Cultural and social uses: Animals used in ceremonies, festivals, and as dowry.


Factors Affecting Distribution of Farm Animals in Nigeria
  1. Climate: Rainfall, temperature, humidity.

  2. Water and feed availability: Adequate grazing land or feed supply.

  3. Disease and pests: Animals are more common where diseases are controlled.

  4. Cultural and religious practices: Some communities avoid certain animals.

  5. Breed adaptability: Some animals are better suited to certain environments.

  6. Infrastructure: Availability of markets, transport, and veterinary services.
