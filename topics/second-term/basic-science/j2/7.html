BOILING AND EVAPORATION USING KINETIC THEORY



Kinetic Theory : This theory explains the behavior of particles in matter. According to the kinetic theory, particles are in constant motion, and the temperature of a substance is a measure of the average kinetic energy of its particles.




EVAPORATION

Evaporation is the process where molecules at the surface of a liquid gain enough energy to enter the gas phase.


Kinetic Energy in Evaporation :
  • Molecules at the surface of a liquid are not all moving at the same speed; some have more kinetic energy than others.

  • If a surface molecule has enough kinetic energy, it can overcome the attractive forces holding it in the liquid and escape into the air as a gas.

  • Higher temperatures increase the average kinetic energy of molecules, making it easier for more molecules to escape, thus increasing the rate of evaporation.

  • When high-energy molecules leave the liquid, the average kinetic energy (temperature) of the remaining molecules decreases, causing the liquid to cool down.
BOILING

Boiling is the process where a liquid turns into a gas not just at the surface, but throughout the liquid, forming bubbles of vapor that rise to the surface and release gas.


Kinetic Energy in Boiling:
  • As a liquid is heated, the kinetic energy of all its molecules increases.

  • At the boiling point, the average kinetic energy of the molecules is high enough that molecules throughout the liquid can overcome the attractive forces holding them in the liquid.

  • When the kinetic energy is sufficient, vapor bubbles form within the liquid. These bubbles rise to the surface and release gas into the air.

  • During boiling, the temperature of the liquid remains constant because the added energy is used to convert the liquid into gas rather than increasing the temperature.
COMPARISON OF EVAPORATION AND BOILING
Evaporation Boiling
1. Occurs only at the surface of the liquid. 1. Occurs throughout the entire liquid.
2. Involves individual molecules with higher kinetic energy escaping from the surface. 2. Involves the entire liquid reaching a temperature where the average kinetic energy allows for vapor bubbles to form.
3. Can happen at any temperature, but is faster at higher temperatures. 3. Happens at a specific temperature (boiling point) for each substance, depending on the atmospheric pressure.
