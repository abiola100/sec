DRUG ABUSE
Meaning of Drug Abus

Drug abuse refers to the improper use of drugs for non-medical purposes, often leading to harmful effects on the body and mind.

It involves:
  • Taking drugs without a doctor's prescription.

  • Using drugs for purposes other than medical treatment.

  • Consuming drugs in excessive amounts.




Types of Drugs Commonly Abused
  1. Narcotics: Pain-relieving drugs that can induce euphoria and are highly addictive. Examples include: Heroin, Morphine, Codeine

  2. Stimulants: Drugs that increase alertness and energy but can lead to health complications. Examples include: Cocaine, Amphetamines

  3. Sedatives: Depressants that slow down brain activity, often leading to drowsiness or unconsciousness. Examples include: Barbiturates, Valium

  4. Hallucinogens: Substances that alter perception and can cause hallucinations. Examples include: LSD (Lysergic acid diethylamide), Psilocybin mushrooms

  5. Cannabis: Often referred to as marijuana or Indian hemp, it is commonly abused for its psychoactive effects.



Causes of Drug Abuse
  1. Peer Pressure: Influence from friends or social groups encouraging drug use.

  2. Curiosity: Desire to experience new sensations or fit in.

  3. Family History: Genetic predisposition or family members who abuse drugs.

  4. Stress and Emotional Issues: Using drugs to cope with anxiety, depression, or trauma.

  5. Availability: Easy access to drugs in the community or household.



Effects of Drug Abuse
Physical Effects:

  • Damaged Organs: Liver, kidneys, and heart can suffer from prolonged drug use.

  • Respiratory Issues: Difficulty in breathing, chronic cough, or lung diseases.

  • Overdose: Risk of fatal overdose leading to death.


Mental and Emotional Effects:

  • Addiction: Compulsive drug-seeking behavior and inability to stop using.

  • Mental Disorders: Anxiety, depression, paranoia, or hallucinations.

  • Cognitive Impairment: Memory loss, poor concentration, and decision-making skills.


Social and Behavioral Effects:

  • Isolation: Withdrawal from family and friends.

  • Poor Academic Performance: Decline in school grades and attendance.

  • Engaging in Risky Behaviors: Unprotected sex, driving under the influence, or criminal activities.



Prevention of Drug Abuse
  1. Education: Learning about the dangers of drug abuse and making informed choices.

  2. Healthy Coping Mechanisms: Developing skills to manage stress, anxiety, and peer pressure.

  3. Strong Support Systems: Building relationships with family, friends, and mentors who encourage positive behaviors.

  4. Engagement in Activities: Participating in sports, arts, or community service to stay occupied and fulfilled.

  5. Seeking Help: Consulting healthcare professionals or counselors when feeling overwhelmed or tempted.
