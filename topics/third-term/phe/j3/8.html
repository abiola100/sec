DISEASES VECTORS

A vector is a living organism that carries and spreads disease-causing germs without getting sick itself.


Common disease vectors include:

  • Mosquitoes – spread malaria and yellow fever.

  • Houseflies – spread cholera, dysentery, typhoid.

  • Tsetse flies – spread sleeping sickness.

  • Blackflies – spread river blindness.

  • Fleas and lice – spread plague and typhus.



life cycles of the vectors
  • Mosquito: Lays eggs in stagnant water → hatches into larvae → changes to pupae → becomes an adult mosquito that bites and spreads disease.

  • Housefly: Lays eggs on dirty things → turns into maggots → changes to pupae → becomes an adult fly that spreads germs on food.

  • Tsetse fly: Gives birth to larva → buries itself and becomes a pupa → grows into an adult fly that bites and spreads sleeping sickness.

  • Blackfly: Lays eggs near fast-flowing water → becomes larva → changes to pupa → becomes an adult blackfly that spreads river blindness.

  • Flea: Lays eggs on animals or beds → becomes larva → changes to pupa → becomes an adult flea that bites and spreads diseases like plague.



control measures for each of the major disease vectors
  • Mosquito: Clear stagnant water around the home, use mosquito nets and insecticides, and wear protective clothing at night.

  • Housefly: Keep the environment clean, cover food properly, dispose of waste quickly, and close doors and windows with nets.

  • Tsetse fly: Clear bushes around homes and farms, avoid areas where tsetse flies live, and wear long-sleeved clothing.

  • Blackfly: Avoid living or bathing near fast-flowing rivers, spray insecticides near riverbanks, and wear protective clothing.

  • Flea: Keep animals and bedding clean, treat pets with flea powder, and clean the home regularly, especially dark corners.



Prevention and Control of Diseases in the Family
  • Wash hands regularly with soap and water.

  • Keep the house and surroundings clean.

  • Use mosquito nets and insect repellents.

  • Avoid sharing personal items like towels or razors.

  • Cook food properly and store it well.

  • Boil drinking water or use clean sources.

  • Cover food to prevent flies.

  • Take sick members to the hospital early.

  • Get vaccinated against common diseases.

  • Control family size using family planning



Roles of Family Members in Health Care
  • Father – provides money, helps with decision-making, and ensures good living conditions.

  • Mother – prepares nutritious meals, keeps the home clean, and cares for the children.

  • Children – follow hygiene rules, help with chores, and keep their personal space clean.

  • Everyone – works together to prevent disease and care for each other.



Personal and Environmental Hygiene
  • Bath daily and wear clean clothes.

  • Brush your teeth and wash your hands often.

  • Sweep the house and compound daily.

  • Dispose of refuse properly.

  • Clear gutters and stagnant water.

  • Wash plates and cover food properly.



Nutrition and Feeding in the Family

Healthy eating helps prevent both infectious and non-infectious diseases.

  • Eat meals rich in protein, vitamins, carbohydrates, and minerals.

  • Give babies breast milk and balanced weaning food.

  • Avoid too much sugar, salt, or fatty foods.

  • Store and cook food properly to avoid food poisoning.



Family Planning and Family Size

Family planning means choosing the number of children you can take care of and spacing them properly.

It helps:

  • Reduce pressure on income and food.

  • Prevent unwanted pregnancies.

  • Ensure better care for each child.

  • Keep the mother healthy.


planning methods :

Family planning methods include natural methods, pills, injections, condoms, and IUDs (only under health guidance).



Caring for the Sick in the Family
When someone is sick at home:

  • Show love and emotional support.

  • Keep the person clean and comfortable.

  • Give medicine as prescribed by the doctor.

  • Take them to the hospital when needed.

  • Keep their items separate to avoid spreading infection.

  • Maintain good hygiene around them.



Consequences of Contracting Diseases on the Family
  • The family may spend a lot of money on hospital bills and medicines.

  • Healthy family members may miss work or school to take care of the sick person.

  • The illness may cause fear, stress, or sadness in the family.

  • Other family members may also become infected, especially with communicable diseases.

  • The family may become poorer and weaker if the illness continues or repeats.

  • Plans for progress, savings, or travel may be delayed or cancelled.



Consequences of Contracting Diseases on the Society
  • Many sick individuals from different families reduce the overall strength of the community.

  • Schools, offices, and markets may not function well because people are sick.

  • The government may spend too much money on disease control instead of development.

  • Disease outbreaks can lead to fear, panic, or even lockdowns.

  • Skilled workers may die, leading to lack of doctors, teachers, or leaders.

  • Poverty and hunger may increase if many families lose their sources of income.




Summary


  • Family health means total well-being of every family member.

  • It includes disease prevention, good hygiene, healthy eating, and emotional support.

  • Diseases can be communicable (spread) or non-communicable (not spread).

  • Germs spread through contact, air, water, food, or insects.

  • Disease vectors like mosquitoes and flies can spread dangerous illnesses.

  • Families must work together to stay healthy, care for the sick, and live responsibly.
