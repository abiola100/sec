NON-CENTRALIZED STATES IN PRE-COLONIAL NIGERIA
Inter-Relationships Among Centres of Civilization

Inter-relationships refer to the connections, influences, and interactions that existed between these centres. They include:

  1. Trade Relations: Cities traded goods like sculptures, palm oil, ivory, kola nuts, and textiles.

  2. Cultural Influence – Religion: Traditional religion, Islam, and Christianity spread through trade and migration.

  3. Cultural Influence – Art and Architecture: Benin’s bronze casting and Hausa fortified cities influenced neighbouring regions.

  4. Political and Diplomatic Ties: City-states formed alliances through marriage, diplomacy, tribute, and political networks.

  5. Migration and Settlement: People moved between centres for trade, farming, or safety, causing cultural exchange and settlement.



Differences Among Centres of Civilization

Despite connections, there were notable differences:

Feature Southern Civilizations (Ife, Benin, Oyo) Northern Civilizations (Kano, Sokoto, Katsina) Niger Delta City-States (Calabar, Warri)
Economy Agriculture, craft, palm oil, trade Trade, agriculture, cattle rearing, crafts Fishing, trading palm oil, slaves
Political System Monarchies (Oba, Alaafin) Emirs, Sultanates Chiefs, councils, smaller kingdoms
Religion Traditional Yoruba beliefs, some Islam Predominantly Islam Traditional beliefs, early Christianity
Art and Culture Bronze casting, sculptures, festivals Leatherwork, weaving, Islamic architecture Carving, canoe making, trade crafts
Urbanization Large cities (Benin, Oyo) Walled cities (Kano, Zaria) Coastal trade towns



INTER-RELATIONSHIPS OF SOME CENTRES OF CIVILIZATION IN PRE-COLONIAL NIGERIA
Relationships between peoples from different centres of civilization in pre-colonial Nigeria

Case Study: Ile-Ife / Benin and Kanem-Bornu / Hausa States




Ile-Ife and Benin Relationship

The relationship between Ile-Ife (Yoruba kingdom) and the Benin Kingdom was one of the most important examples of interaction between pre-colonial Nigerian civilizations.

1. Historical Background

  • Ile-Ife is regarded as the spiritual and cultural centre of the Yoruba, seen as the “cradle of Yoruba civilization.”

  • Benin Kingdom was one of the most powerful and organized states in the forest region of Nigeria, ruled by the Oba of Benin.

  • According to oral traditions, Benin traced part of its royal institutions to Ile-Ife. This created cultural and political ties between the two kingdoms.

2. Political Relationship

  • The royal dynasty of Benin is believed to have been influenced by Ile-Ife.

  • A prince from Ife, sometimes identified as Oranmiyan, was invited to Benin to establish a stable monarchy.

  • His descendants became the Obas of Benin.

  • This created a permanent link between the two kingdoms through dynastic origin.

  • Diplomatic ties were maintained through exchange of gifts, envoys, and political alliances.

3. Economic Relationship

  • Both centres engaged in trade and exchange of goods.

  • Ile-Ife was famous for its artistic works (terracotta, bronze, and stone sculptures).

  • Benin developed its own bronze casting industry, influenced by Ife art.

  • They exchanged agricultural products, ivory, beads, and slaves.

  • The trade routes that linked Ile-Ife and Benin also connected them with other Yoruba towns and coastal areas, boosting regional commerce.

4. Cultural Relationship

  • Artistic Influence: Ife’s advanced bronze and terracotta art strongly influenced Benin’s renowned bronze casting tradition.

  • Religion: Both shared belief in a supreme deity and worship of ancestors.

  • Rituals, coronation practices, and festivals in Benin reflected Yoruba traditions adopted from Ife.

  • Language Influence: Elements of Yoruba culture, titles, and traditions entered Benin society.

5. Social Relationship

  • Intermarriages occurred between the ruling families and nobles of the two kingdoms.

  • Migration of artisans, traders, and priests between Ile-Ife and Benin helped blend cultures.

  • This exchange promoted unity and mutual respect between the two centres.



Significance of the Ile-Ife and Benin Relationship

  • Helped spread Yoruba culture and traditions into Benin.

  • Promoted unity and cultural exchange between two powerful pre-colonial states.

  • Strengthened Benin’s monarchy, giving it political stability.

  • Gave rise to the rich artistic traditions of Benin, which became famous across West Africa.

  • Enhanced economic prosperity through trade networks.



Kanem-Bornu and Hausa States — Inter-relationships & Differences

Introduction

Before colonial rule, Nigeria was home to powerful and organized states such as the Kanem-Bornu Empire (in the North-East) and the Hausa States (in the North-West). These centres of civilization developed rich political, economic, social, and cultural systems. Though different in origin, they were not isolated — they interacted through trade, religion, culture, migration, and warfare.

Kanem-Bornu Empire

  • Located in the Lake Chad region, with capital at Ngazargamu.

  • One of the oldest states in the area, existing from the 9th century.

  • Rulers were known as Mai.

  • Strongly influenced by Islam from the 11th century.

  • Economy: farming (millet, sorghum), fishing, and trans-Saharan trade in slaves, salt, horses, and kola nuts.

Hausa States

  • Group of city-states such as Kano, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir, Daura, Rano, Biram.

  • Each ruled by a Sarki (king).

  • Known for trans-Saharan trade, crafts, leatherwork, cloth weaving, and iron-smelting.

  • Islam spread widely in the Hausa states from the 14th century.



Areas of Inter-relationship between Kanem-Bornu and Hausa States

(a) Trade

  • Both were part of the trans-Saharan trade network.

  • Kanem-Bornu supplied slaves, ostrich feathers, ivory, and horses.

  • Hausa states supplied leather, cloth, dyed materials, grains, and salt.

  • Kano and Katsina became commercial centres attracting Kanem-Bornu traders.



(b) Religion

  • Islam was a major link between the regions.

  • Both regions embraced Islam, encouraging closer ties.

  • Muslim scholars and clerics moved between Kanem-Bornu and Hausa cities, spreading Islamic learning and law.

  • Pilgrimage routes to Mecca passed through both areas, strengthening contact.



(c) Culture

  • Islam influenced language (Arabic writing), dress, education, and architecture in both regions.

  • Hausa states borrowed Islamic judicial systems (Sharia) which Kanem-Bornu had earlier adopted.

  • Architectural styles (mosques, palaces, city walls) showed similarities due to Islamic influence.



(d) Politics and Diplomacy

  • Kanem-Bornu was at times more powerful and influenced Hausa states through military strength and diplomacy.

  • Hausa rulers sometimes sought alliances with Bornu for protection against rivals.

  • In later years, Bornu kings exchanged ambassadors and gifts with Hausa rulers.



(e) Migration and Settlement

  • Traders, scholars, and migrants moved freely between the two regions.

  • Hausa traders settled in Bornu towns, while Bornu traders lived in Kano and Katsina.

  • These movements led to intermarriages and blending of cultures.



Significance of the Inter-relationships

  • Strengthened economic growth of both regions.

  • Promoted the spread of Islam and Arabic literacy.

  • Enhanced cultural exchange (art, dress, architecture, festivals).

  • Improved diplomatic and political relations.

  • Helped create unity of identity in Northern Nigeria before colonialism.



Differences between Kanem-Bornu and Hausa States
Feature Kanem-Bornu Empire Hausa States
Political System Centralized under a powerful Mai Decentralized into many independent Sarkis
Religion Islam became dominant earlier (11th century) Islam spread later (14th century)
Military Strength Strong, with cavalry and foreign alliances Weaker, often invaded by stronger neighbours
Economy Fishing, farming, trans-Saharan trade Trade (Kano, Katsina), crafts, weaving



DIFFERENCES AMONG CENTRES OF CIVILIZATION IN PRE-COLONIAL NIGERIA

The major centres of civilization in pre-colonial Nigeria were:

  • Yoruba Kingdoms (Ile-Ife, Oyo)

  • Benin Kingdom

  • Igbo societies

  • Hausa States

  • Kanem-Bornu Empire

  • Niger Delta States (Bonny, Opobo, Warri, Calabar)

Though they interacted through trade, war, and migration, they had clear differences in political structure, culture, and religion.

1 POLITICAL STRUCTURE

Yoruba Kingdoms (Ile-Ife, Oyo)

  • Centralized monarchy.

  • Ooni of Ife was both political and spiritual leader.


  • Alaafin of Oyo had political authority, assisted by the Oyo Mesi (council of chiefs) and Ogboni society.

  • Oyo Empire was well organized with provinces and tribute collection.

Benin Kingdom

  • Highly centralized monarchy.

  • The Oba of Benin was supreme ruler, regarded as divine.

  • Assisted by chiefs (Uzama nobles, palace chiefs, and town chiefs).

  • Strong military structure to control territories.

Igbo Societies

  • Decentralized, acephalous (stateless).

  • No single king in most areas.

  • Decisions taken by council of elders, age grades, and secret societies (e.g., Ekpe, Okonko).

  • Some exceptions like Onitsha and Nri which had kings.

Hausa States

  • Each city-state (Kano, Katsina, Zaria, Gobir, etc.) ruled by a Sarki (king).

  • Later replaced by Emirs after the Fulani Jihad (19th century).

  • Strong influence of Islamic scholars and officials in governance.



Kanem-Bornu Empire

  • One of the oldest centralized kingdoms.

  • Ruler was the Mai, powerful and respected.

  • Strong administrative system with nobles, governors, and army leaders.



Niger Delta States

  • Small city-states.

  • Ruled by kings (Obong, Amanyanabo, Olu) and councils of elders.

  • Traders and canoe-house leaders played important roles in politics.



2. CULTURE AND CUSTOMS

Yoruba

  • Rich festivals: Olojo, Ogun, Sango, Egungun.

  • Respect for kingship and ancestors.

  • Known for terracotta and bronze art.

  • Marriage: dowry and elaborate ceremonies.

Benin

  • Elaborate palace rituals.

  • Art: famous for bronze casting and ivory carving.

  • Oba seen as semi-divine.

  • Strong guild traditions.

Igbo

  • Age-grade system very important.

  • Masquerades (Mmanwu) and New Yam Festival.

  • Strong respect for ancestors.

  • Marriage: bride price negotiations.

Hausa

  • Culture influenced by Islam.

  • Horse-riding, Durbar festivals.

  • Craftsmanship in leather, cloth dyeing, weaving.

  • Marriage based on Islamic laws.

Kanem-Bornu

  • Strong Islamic scholarship.

  • Use of Arabic language in learning.

  • Military culture: horsemen and cavalry.

  • Customs shaped by Islam.

Niger Delta

  • Fishing and canoe culture.

  • Festivals tied to water spirits and deities.

  • Trade customs and hospitality to foreigners.

  • Marriage often linked with dowries in goods.

3. RELIGIOUS BELIEFS

Yoruba

  • Believed in Olodumare (Supreme God).

  • Worship of Orishas (e.g., Ogun, Sango, Ifa).

  • Priests, diviners (babalawo) played important roles.

Benin

  • Believed in Osanobua (Supreme God).

  • Worship of ancestors and other deities.

  • Oba acted as chief priest and religious head.

Igbo

  • Believed in Chukwu (Supreme God).

  • Worship of deities (e.g., Amadioha, Ala).

  • Ancestor worship central.
  • Divination through oracles (e.g., Arochukwu oracle).

Hausa

  • Islam became dominant from the 14th century.

  • Islamic law (Sharia) guided life.

  • Muslims observed daily prayers, fasting, and pilgrimage.

Kanem-Bornu

  • Islam adopted earlier (11th century).

  • Famous for Islamic scholarship, Qur’anic schools, and mosques.

  • Rulers promoted Islam as state religion.

Niger Delta

  • Traditional beliefs in water spirits (Mammy Water).

  • Worship of ancestors and local deities.

  • From the 15th century, Christianity spread through Portuguese and missionaries.

4. SUMMARY

  • Political Structure: Centralized kingdoms (Oyo, Benin, Kanem-Bornu) vs decentralized societies (Igbo).

  • Culture and Customs: Differences in festivals, art, marriage customs, social systems (age grades, chiefs, guilds).

  • Religious Beliefs: Yoruba, Igbo, Benin = Traditional religion with Supreme God + lesser gods; Hausa and Kanem-Bornu = Islam; Niger Delta = Traditional religion + early Christianity.

Thus, pre-colonial Nigeria was diverse in politics, culture, and religion, which shaped the identity of its people before colonialism.
