SQUARE AND SQUARE ROOT TABLE

INTRODUCTION

In mathematics, there are certain operations that we perform repeatedly. One of them is multiplication. When we multiply a number by itself, the result we get is called a square number. The process of multiplying a number by itself is called squaring the number.

The opposite of squaring a number is finding its square root. The square root of a number is the number that, when multiplied by itself, gives that number.

The study of square and square roots is very important in mathematics because it helps us to understand many other topics such as algebra, geometry, mensuration, Pythagoras’ theorem, area of squares, and even real-life calculations.

Before the use of calculators and computers, students used square and square root tables to find values of squares and square roots quickly. These tables are still useful for understanding how these concepts work.

SQUARE NUMBERS

A square number (or a perfect square) is the product of a number multiplied by itself.

This means: Square of a number = number × number

For example:

  1. The square of 1 = 1 × 1 = 1

  2. The square of 2 = 2 × 2 = 4

  3. The square of 3 = 3 × 3 = 9

  4. The square of 4 = 4 × 4 = 16

  5. The square of 5 = 5 × 5 = 25

  6. The square of 6 = 6 × 6 = 36

  7. The square of 7 = 7 × 7 = 49

  8. The square of 8 = 8 × 8 = 64

  9. The square of 9 = 9 × 9 = 81

  10. The square of 10 = 10 × 10 = 100

When we write “2 squared,” we write it as 2².
When we write “5 squared,” we write it as 5².
The small number written at the top right (²) is called a power or index.

So, when a number has ² beside it, it means that number has been multiplied by itself once.

SQUARE ROOTS

The square root of a number is the value that, when multiplied by itself, gives the original number.

For example:

  1. √4 = 2 because 2 × 2 = 4

  2. √9 = 3 because 3 × 3 = 9

  3. √16 = 4 because 4 × 4 = 16

  4. √25 = 5 because 5 × 5 = 25

  5. √49 = 7 because 7 × 7 = 49

The symbol used to represent the square root is called the radical sign (√). So, √36 = 6, and 6² = 36.

Squaring and finding the square root are opposite operations. If we square a number, we get a bigger value. If we find its square root, we get back the original smaller value.

PERFECT SQUARES

A perfect square is a number that is the exact square of a whole number. Its square root is always a whole number (that means it does not have decimal parts).

For example:

  1. 1 = 1 × 1

  2. 4 = 2 × 2

  3. 9 = 3 × 3

  4. 16 = 4 × 4

  5. 25 = 5 × 5

  6. 36 = 6 × 6

  7. 49 = 7 × 7

  8. 64 = 8 × 8

  9. 81 = 9 × 9

  10. 100 = 10 × 10

That means √1 = 1, √4 = 2, √9 = 3, √16 = 4, and so on.

Perfect squares are very easy to work with because their square roots are neat whole numbers.

NON-PERFECT SQUARES

Some numbers are not perfect squares. That means their square roots are not whole numbers but decimal or irrational numbers.

For example:

  1. √2 = 1.4142…

  2. √3 = 1.732…

  3. √5 = 2.236…

  4. √6 = 2.449…

  5. √7 = 2.646…

  6. √8 = 2.828…

  7. √10 = 3.162…

These numbers go on without ending, and they do not repeat in any pattern. That is why they are called irrational numbers.

We can only approximate their square roots (that means write them in short decimal form).

HOW TO USE THE SQUARE ROOT TABLE

When you want to find the square root of a number that is not a perfect square (like 50), do the following:

  1. Look for the two nearest perfect squares around it. For 50, the nearest perfect squares are 49 and 64.

  2. Find their square roots. √49 = 7, √64 = 8.

  3. Since 50 is between 49 and 64, √50 will be between 7 and 8.

  4. Because 50 is closer to 49, its square root will be closer to 7. So √50 ≈ 7.07

METHODS OF FINDING SQUARE ROOTS

There are several ways to find square roots:

  1. By Using Square Root Table – For numbers not easily squared.

  2. By Factorization Method – For perfect squares.

  3. By Long Division Method – For large or non-perfect square numbers.

  4. By Estimation – For rough answers or mental calculation.

(a) Factorization Method

To find the square root of a perfect square number by factorization:

  1. Break the number into its prime factors.

  2. Group the factors in pairs.

  3. Take one number from each pair and multiply them to get the square root.

Example 1: Find √144

144 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 3

Group into pairs: (2×2), (2×2), (3×3)

Take one number from each pair: 2 × 2 × 3 = 12

So √144 = 12

Example 2: Find √400

400 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 2 × 5 × 5

= (2×2) (2×2) (5×5)

Take one number from each pair: 2 × 2 × 5 = 20

√400 = 20

(b) Long Division Method

This method is used for larger numbers or non-perfect squares. It is a bit like ordinary long division but in a special pattern.

Steps (simplified):

  1. Starting from the right, group the digits of the number in pairs.

  2. Find the largest number whose square is less than or equal to the first group.

  3. Subtract its square and bring down the next group.

  4. Double the quotient (the current root) and find a suitable digit to complete the divisor.

  5. Continue the process until you get the required accuracy.

IMPORTANCE OF SQUARE AND SQUARE ROOTS

Learning about square and square roots helps us to:

  1. Find the area of a square.

  2. Solve algebraic equations.

  3. Use Pythagoras’ theorem to find missing sides of right-angled triangles.

  4. Estimate distances and areas in real life.

  5. Improve our ability to work without calculators.

  6. Understand higher-level mathematics such as indices and surds.

Example: If the area of a square field is 625 m², then the length of one side is √625 = 25 m.
This kind of reasoning is used in construction, land measurement, and science.

COMMON MISTAKES STUDENTS MAKE

  1. Confusing the meaning of square and square root.

  2. Forgetting that √ means finding a number that multiplies by itself.

  3. Trying to find the square root of a negative number (not possible in basic mathematics).

  4. Using wrong values from tables.

  5. Forgetting that squaring and taking square roots are opposite operations.

SUMMARY

  1. The square of a number = number × number.

  2. The square root of a number = the number that gives it when multiplied by itself.

  3. Perfect squares have whole number roots.

  4. Non-perfect squares have decimal or irrational roots.

  5. Square and square root tables help to find these values quickly.

  6. We can find square roots by factorization, long division, or estimation.

CONCLUSION

The study of squares and square roots is one of the most important topics in junior secondary mathematics. It builds the foundation for advanced topics in senior classes.

It also helps us solve everyday problems involving areas, distances, and estimations. By learning to use square and square root tables, students become faster and more confident in solving mathematical problems, especially when calculators are not allowed.

Understanding this topic prepares students for future mathematical studies and real-life applications.




Square and Square Root Examples

Example 1:

A square garden has an area of 784 m². Find the length of one side of the garden.

Solution:

Area of a square = side × side = side²

So, side² = 784

Find the square root of 784:

√784 = 28

Therefore, each side of the garden is 28 metres long.


Example 2:

Find the area of a square tile whose side is 15 cm.

Solution:

Area = side × side = 15 × 15 = 225 cm²

The area of the tile is 225 cm².


Example 3:

The area of a square plate is 961 cm². Find the perimeter of the plate.

Solution:

side² = 961

√961 = 31

Perimeter of a square = 4 × side = 4 × 31 = 124 cm

Therefore, the perimeter = 124 cm.


Example 4:

A farmer wants to fence a square piece of land of 900 m². Find the total length of fencing wire he needs.

Solution:

side² = 900

√900 = 30

Perimeter = 4 × side = 4 × 30 = 120 m

The farmer needs 120 metres of wire.


Example 5:

Find the square root of 529 by factorization method.

Solution:

529 = 23 × 23 = (23 × 23)

Group factors: √(23 × 23) = 23

Therefore, √529 = 23.

(Or using factorization: 529 ÷ 23 = 23)


Example 6:

Find the square root of 784 by long division method.

Solution (Step-by-step):

Group digits in pairs from right to left → (7)(84)

Find the largest number whose square is ≤ 7 → that is 2 (2² = 4).

Subtract 4 from 7 → remainder 3. Bring down 84 → 384.

Double 2 → 4, now find a digit x such that 4x × x ≤ 384.

46 × 6 = 276

47 × 7 = 329

48 × 8 = 384

Therefore, √784 = 28.

The square root of 784 is 28.


Example 7:

Estimate √300 to one decimal place.

Solution:

The two nearest perfect squares to 300 are 289 and 324.

√289 = 17, √324 = 18

√300 lies between 17 and 18.

Now, 300 is closer to 289 than 324, so √300 ≈ 17.3

Therefore, √300 ≈ 17.3 (1 d.p.)



Example 8:

If the side of a square is increased from 12 cm to 15 cm, by how much does the area increase?

Solution:

Original area = 12 × 12 = 144 cm²

New area = 15 × 15 = 225 cm²

Increase = 225 − 144 = 81 cm²

Therefore, the area increases by 81 cm².


Example 9:

The square of a number is 1225. Find the number.

Solution:

Let the number be n.

Then, n² = 1225

Take square root of both sides:

n = √1225 = 35

The number is 35.


Example 10:

A square pool has a side of 18 m. Find:
(a) Its area, and
(b) Its diagonal length (correct to 1 decimal place).

Solution:

(a) Area = side² = 18² = 324 m²
(b) Diagonal = side × √2 = 18 × 1.414 = 25.5 m

Therefore, area = 324 m² and diagonal = 25.5 m.
