HUMAN TRAFFICKING
Meaning of Human Trafficking

Human trafficking is the act of recruiting, transporting, or transferring a person across borders, either within Nigeria or to another country, with the intention to deceive and exploit them. This can include forcing them into work, prostitution, or other forms of abuse.



Factors Responsible for Human Trafficking
  1. False hope of education or work abroad: Many children and women are tricked with promises of school or jobs.

  2. Poverty: Lack of money forces people to accept risky opportunities.

  3. High demand for foreign workers: People travel seeking jobs, making them vulnerable.

  4. Growth of sex and entertainment industries: Traffickers exploit these industries to recruit victims.

  5. Weak family love and guidance: Children or women without care or supervision are easier to exploit.

  6. Billion-dollar trafficking business: The large profits motivate traffickers to continue their crimes.



Consequences of Human Trafficking
  1. Loss of human resources: Victims are removed from school or work, reducing skilled people in society.

  2. Life of crime: Some trafficked individuals turn to crime for survival.

  3. Addiction: Victims may be forced to take drugs, leading to dependency.

  4. Sexual violence: Victims often face rape, abuse, and exploitation.

  5. Psychological trauma: Victims experience fear, depression, and stress.

  6. Weakness of family influence: Families lose their role in guiding and protecting children.

  7. Spread of Diseases: Exploited individuals may be exposed to sexually transmitted infections and other health risks.

  8. Social Stigma: Victims may face discrimination and rejection upon return to their communities.

  9. Economic Exploitation: Trafficked individuals are often forced to work without pay or underpaid, contributing to economic disparities.




Preventive Measures of Human Trafficking
  1. Awareness: Teach people, especially children and women, about the dangers of trafficking.

  2. Education: Encourage schooling and inform students about safe ways to work or travel.

  3. Advocacy: Community leaders and organizations should speak against trafficking.

  4. Legislation: Follow and enforce laws against human trafficking.

  5. Family involvement: Families should provide love, care, and guidance to children.

  6. Monitoring travel opportunities: Check job offers abroad carefully to avoid being tricked.



Agencies and Laws Against Human Trafficking

Various measures have been implemented to address and reduce human trafficking:

  1. National Agency for the Prohibition of Trafficking in Persons (NAPTIP): Started in 2003, it fights human trafficking in Nigeria. It helps prevent trafficking, protect victims, and punish traffickers.

  2. Trafficking in Persons (Prohibition) Enforcement and Administration Act: The law makes human trafficking illegal and punishes offenders. It also protects victims.

  3. Awareness Campaigns: Groups teach people about the dangers of trafficking and how to stay safe.

  4. International Cooperation: Nigeria works with other countries to stop trafficking across borders.




Roles of the Family in Preventing Human Trafficking
  1. Teach children about the dangers of trafficking.

  2. Watch for signs that someone may be at risk.

  3. Talk openly with children to build trust.

  4. Give care and support to prevent risky choices.
