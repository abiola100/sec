CROPS

Crops are plants cultivated for human use. They provide food, income, raw materials, and other products essential to society. Understanding crops involves knowing their types, classes, uses, cultivation practices, and the factors that affect growth.

Meaning of Crop

A crop is any plant cultivated for human use. It may be grown for:

  • Food: e.g., yam, maize, rice, cassava

  • Cash or Export: e.g., cocoa, oil palm, cotton

  • Industrial Use: e.g., rubber, sugarcane

  • Medicinal or Herbal Use: e.g., moringa, neem


Classes of Crops

Crops can be classified in different ways:

1 Based on Plant Form

  • Monocotyledonous Plants: Plants with a single seed leaf. Examples: maize, rice, sorghum, millet.

  • Dicotyledonous Plants: Plants with two seed leaves. Examples: beans, cocoa, groundnut, oil palm.

2 Based on Life Span

  • Annual Crops: Complete their life cycle in one year or growing season. Examples: maize, rice, beans.

  • Perennial Crops: Grow and produce for more than one year. Examples: cocoa, mango, oil palm, rubber.

Uses of Crops
  1. Food: Provide carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, and minerals.

  2. Income Generation: Cash crops provide money through sale or export.

  3. Raw Materials for Industry: Many crops are used in manufacturing.

  4. Employment: Farming and processing create jobs.

  5. Medicinal Uses: Certain crops are used in herbal medicine.

  6. Environmental Uses: Trees and crops prevent soil erosion and improve soil fertility.


Factors Affecting Crop Production
  1. Soil: Fertile, well-drained soil is ideal. Soil type affects crop choice.

  2. Climate: Temperature, rainfall, and sunlight influence growth and yield.

  3. Water Availability: Irrigation is needed in areas with low rainfall.

  4. Seed Quality: Use of high-quality seeds increases yield and reduces disease.

  5. Fertilizer and Manure: Nutrient supplementation improves growth.

  6. Pests and Diseases: Proper management reduces crop loss.

  7. Labour and Tools: Availability of labour and farm tools affects timely operations.


Crop Production Practices
  1. Land Preparation: Clearing, ploughing, and leveling the land.

  2. Planting: Using appropriate spacing and depth for seeds or seedlings.

  3. Weeding: Removing unwanted plants that compete for nutrients and water.

  4. Watering/Irrigation: Supplying water during dry periods.

  5. Fertilization: Applying organic or inorganic fertilizers to improve soil fertility.

  6. Pest and Disease Control: Using chemical or biological methods.

  7. Harvesting: Collecting mature crops at the right time to prevent loss.

  8. Post-Harvest Handling: Drying, storage, processing, and packaging to prevent spoilage.


Crop Rotation and Intercropping
  • Crop Rotation: Growing different crops on the same land in successive seasons to maintain soil fertility and reduce pests.

  • Intercropping: Growing two or more crops together to maximize space, reduce pests, and increase yield.
