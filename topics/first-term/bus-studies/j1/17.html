ECONOMIC DIVISION



Economic divisions refer to the various ways an economy is segmented or categorized to understand better and manage its components.

These divisions can be made based on several factors, including economic activities, income levels, and sectors. Here are some key economic divisions:



  1. DIVISION BY ECONOMIC ACTIVITIES


    • Primary production : Activities related to the extraction and harvesting of natural resources. Examples include agriculture, mining, fishing, and forestry.

    • Secondary production : Activities related to manufacturing and processing. This sector transforms raw materials from the primary sector into finished goods. Examples include factories producing cars, clothing, and electronics.

    • Tertiary production : Activities related to providing services. Examples include retail, healthcare, education, banking, and entertainment.

    • Quaternary production : Activities related to intellectual services and information. Examples include research and development, information technology, and consulting.

    • Quinary production : Activities involving high-level decision-making and advanced services. Examples include government roles, top executives, and non-profit management.


  2. DIVISION BY EMPLOYMENT


    • Formal Sector: Jobs with official recognition, legal protection, and regulations. These jobs typically offer benefits such as healthcare, retirement plans, and job security. Examples include government jobs, corporate employment, and registered businesses.

    • Informal Sector : Jobs without formal recognition or legal protection. These jobs often lack benefits and job security. Examples include street vending, unregistered small businesses.


  3. DIVISION BY OWNERSHIP


    • Public Sector : Economic activities and enterprises owned and operated by the government. Examples include public schools, national defense, and state-owned enterprises.

    • Private Sector: Economic activities and enterprises owned and operated by private individuals or companies. Examples include privately-owned businesses, multinational corporations, and private healthcare providers.

    • Voluntary Sector (Non-Profit Sector) : Organizations that operate on a non-profit basis, often providing social services, charity work, and community support. Examples include non-governmental organizations (NGOs), charities, and community groups.


  4. DIVISION BY GEOGRAPHIC AREA


    • Urban Economies : Economies centered in cities and metropolitan areas. These economies often have a high concentration of services, industries, and infrastructure.

    • Rural Economies : Economies centered in non-urban areas, often relying more on agriculture, forestry, and natural resources.
