MATTER

What is matter ?

Matter can be define as anything that has mass and occupies space. It makes up all the physical substances in the universe.


CLASSIFICATION OF MATTER


Physical Classification of Matter
  1. Solids: These have a definite shape and volume. The particles in solids are closely packed together, which is why they maintain their shape. Examples include rocks, wood, and ice.

  2. Liquids: Liquids have a definite volume but take the shape of their container. The particles are close together but can move around, which allows liquids to flow. Examples include water, oil, and milk.

  3. Gases: Gases do not have a definite shape or volume. The particles are far apart and move freely, so gases fill the entire space available to them. Examples include air, helium, and steam.

  4. Plasma: This is a high-energy state of matter where the gas phase is energized until atomic electrons are no longer associated with any particular atomic nucleus. Plasma is found in stars, including the sun, and in neon signs and lightning.
Chemical Classification of Matter
  1. Elements: Pure substances that consist of only one type of atom. Examples include oxygen (O), gold (Au), and carbon (C).

  2. Compounds: Substances made from two or more elements that are chemically bonded. They have fixed proportions and unique properties. Examples include water (H₂O), salt (NaCl), and carbon dioxide (CO₂).

  3. Mixtures: Combinations of two or more substances that are not chemically bonded and can be separated by physical means. They can be further divided into:
    • Homogeneous mixtures (solutions): The composition is uniform throughout. Examples include saltwater and air.

    • Heterogeneous mixtures: The composition is not uniform, and different parts of the mixture can be seen. Examples include salad and sand in water.







STATE OF MATTER
  1. Solids
    2. Characteristics:
    1. Definite shape and volume.

    2. Particles are tightly packed in a fixed, orderly arrangement.

    3. Particles vibrate but do not move from their positions.

    4. Strong intermolecular forces.

      5. Examples: Ice, diamond, iron.



  2. Liquids
    3. Characteristics:
    1. Definite volume but no definite shape; they take the shape of their container.

    2. Particles are close together but not in a fixed arrangement.

    3. Particles can move and flow past one another.

    4. Moderate intermolecular forces.

      5. Examples: Water, oil, mercury.






  3. Gases

    4. Characteristics:
    1. No definite shape or volume; they expand to fill their container.

    2. Particles are far apart and move freely.

    3. Very weak intermolecular forces.

    4. High kinetic energy.
      5. Examples: Oxygen, nitrogen, carbon dioxide.


  4. Plasma

    5. Characteristics:
    1. Ionized gas with free electrons and ions.

    2. Conducts electricity and is affected by magnetic fields.

    3. Very high energy state.

    4. Often found at high temperatures.

      5. Examples: Sun, neon signs, lightning.
